Photography

I take photos, mostly of nature and landscapes. Some of my photos won awards in competitions.

I am always happy to give permission for my photos to be used for a good cause. If you are a non-profit and would like to use one of my photos to promote your work, an educational or conservation project, please get in touch with me.

If you want to buy one of my photos - either as a print or for digital use, send me a message and we can work out details.

Awarded photographs

11.2020

Photo Breath. Adapt. Relax. selected as the Category Winner of the category ‘Up Close and Personal’ of the Capturing Ecology 2020 Photo Competition organised by the British Ecological Society.

A bearded leaf chameleon, with its rather underwhelming ‘beard’ consisting of just a few raised scales. The species is endemic to the Eastern Arc mountains of Tanzania and Kenya. This one was spotted during a night walk in the Udzungwa mountains in Tanzania.

11.2020

Photo Take no prisoners noted as Highly Commended in the category ‘Dynamic Ecosystems’ of the Capturing Ecology 2020 Photo Competition organised by the British Ecological Society.

Matabele ants (Megaponera analis) is a monotypic genus of ants named after the brave warriors of Matabele tribe from modern-day Zimbabwe. They and highly specialised termite predators. Raiding columns of workers are sent to find and plunder termite nests like this one.

12.2018 Photo The Gathering selected as Category Winner in the ‘Niches and Hollows: Adaptive Behaviour & Australian Biodiversity’ category as well as Overall Best Image at the Ecological Society of Australia Ecology in Action 2018 Photo Competition

Egrets in first morning sun on a small rocky island on the Clarence River near Copmanhurst, NSW, Australia.

11.2018
Photo An early physical noted as Highly Commended in the category ‘Ecology in Action’ of the Capturing Ecology 2018 Photo Competition organised by the British Ecological Society.

Spotted-tail quolls (Dasyurus maculatus) have a gestation period of just 21 days. When born, they are only 7mm long. After that, like in all marsupials, young continue to grow inside mothers pouch for around 12 weeks, giving researches unique chance to study them at this very early stage.

11.2017
Photo Have you had your annual dental check-up selected as Category Winner in the ‘Out Standing in the Field’ category at the Ecological Society of Australia Ecology in Action 2017 Photo Competition

A PhD candidate, Fran Zewe, performing a dental check on a sedated male feral cat caught as a part of her PhD research.
